# Hashtag reality

The honest version: hashtags are mostly categorisation, not a
distribution lever. The old "30 tags for reach" playbook is dead on
Instagram, noise on TikTok, and a risk on YouTube. Use a small number
of niche-specific tags per platform and put the SEO effort into the
caption and the on-screen text instead.

## Instagram

- Mosseri has stated directly that hashtags **do not improve reach**.
  They function as labels for search, not as a distribution multiplier.
- The "follow hashtag" feature is gone, which removed hashtags as a
  discovery channel.
- Current guidance: **3 to 5 highly relevant hashtags**, niche, not
  generic.
- Instagram caps hashtag count: piling on more does not help and gets
  flagged as keyword stuffing.
- Putting hashtags in the first comment instead of the caption no
  longer hides them from the count.

**What earns the slot:** specific, audience-aligned tags. `#retinolroutine`,
not `#skincare`. `#sourdoughtroubleshooting`, not `#bread`.

**What wastes it:** `#viral`, `#explore`, `#instagood`, `#love`, anything
that's attached to a billion posts.

## TikTok

- Hashtags still feed the algorithm as a categorisation and SEO input,
  but generic hashtags are noise.
- `#fyp`, `#foryou`, `#viral`, `#trending` provide essentially zero
  algorithmic benefit. They're attached to billions of videos.
- Working formula: **3 to 5 niche-specific hashtags**, optionally with
  one trending or topical tag if it genuinely fits.
- Long-tail beats short. `#dryskinroutine` carries an SEO signal.
  `#skincare` does not.
- CapCut's own guidance (ByteDance-owned) lands in the same place:
  three to five, niche, relevant.

**Working mix:** 1 to 2 topical or trending tags that actually match
the content, plus 2 to 3 niche tags. Stop there.

## YouTube Shorts

- Hashtags act as topic and category signals.
- Put **3 to 5 hashtags in the description**, not in the title. The
  first three description hashtags auto-appear above the title as
  clickable links anyway, so burning title characters on them is
  wasted space.
- **`#Shorts` is the one required hashtag.** It signals to YouTube
  that the video is eligible for the Shorts feed.
- Hard cap: **15 hashtags total per video**. Go above 15 and YouTube
  ignores all of them. Absolute ceiling is 60 across all metadata
  before active penalty.

**Working pattern:** `#Shorts` + 2 to 4 niche tags in the description.
Done.

## Branded hashtags

Still useful, but as a **navigation device**, not a reach play. A
viewer can tap your branded hashtag from your profile and see every
post tagged with it. For UGC campaigns, it's how you collect user
posts. Treat it as a search filter, not a discovery multiplier.

## The death of "trending hashtags" as a discovery tool

The old advice to chase trending hashtags for reach is obsolete on
Instagram. On TikTok and Shorts, trending hashtags still matter
modestly, but only when topically relevant. Slapping a trend tag on
unrelated content reads as noise to the algorithm and to viewers.

## What to do with the energy you used to spend on hashtags

Put it into the caption and the on-screen text. The caption is the SEO
on Instagram now. The on-screen text is what the OCR reads on TikTok
and what the muted viewer reads on every platform. See `caption-craft.md`
and `on-screen-text.md`.

## Quick reference

| Platform | Count | Where | Required | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Instagram | 3 to 5 | Caption | None | `#viral` `#explore` `#fyp`, 30-tag walls |
| TikTok | 3 to 5 | Caption | None | `#fyp` `#foryou` `#viral` `#trending` |
| YouTube Shorts | 3 to 5 | Description | `#Shorts` | More than 15 total (all ignored) |
