# Pinned comments

The pinned comment is a second surface most creators leave empty. It
extends watch time, answers the obvious question before it's asked,
carries the link or list that didn't fit, and seeds the direction of
the comment section. On Instagram especially, it's one of the cheapest edits you
can make to a published post for the lift it delivers.

## Per-platform reality

### Instagram (the strong case)

- You can pin **your own** comments, up to **three per post or Reel**.
- Ordering quirk: when three are pinned, the most recently pinned shows
  first and the oldest shows third. Order matters.
- The pinned comment is highly visible: it sits above the rest and
  often shows in the comment preview on the feed.
- Pin from your own account, immediately after publishing, before the
  comment section fills up.

### TikTok (the awkward case)

- The "pin your own comment" feature has not been broadly available.
  Treat it as if it isn't there.
- The working substitute: **reply with a video**. Your video reply to a
  selected comment sticks to the top of the comment section and creates
  a second piece of content tied to the original. Use this both as a
  pin substitute and as a content engine.
- A weaker tactic: race to be the **first commenter** on your own video.
  TikTok briefly displays the first comment in a highlighted state.
  Fragile, not a real substitute.

### YouTube Shorts

- The creator's pinned comment carries a "Pinned by [channel]" badge.
- The combined **heart + pin** combo is the standard play. The heart
  notifies the commenter and lifts engagement on that thread.
- Pin from your own account with a question, the link to the full
  video, or context that extends watch time.

## What to put in the pinned comment

Four patterns, ordered by how often they pay off:

### 1. Second hook / context extension

A short line that answers the obvious question the viewer was about to
type. Lengthens watch time because viewers read the pin while the
video loops, or they re-watch with the pin's context in mind.

Examples:

- Under a "why your retinol is peeling" video: "Day 14 update: still
  no flaking, and I haven't dropped the strength."
- Under a "best espresso under $300" video: "Yes, the cheaper one
  beat the $800 one on shot consistency too."

### 2. Objection-killer

Pre-empt the predictable critical comment. Take it off the board so it
doesn't dominate the section.

Examples:

- "Yes, this works on oily skin too. I tested both."
- "Before you say 'sponsored': I bought all three."
- "Not affiliate, not paid. The brand doesn't know I exist."

### 3. Link / list

The product list, the link, the recipe, the longer breakdown that
didn't fit cleanly in the caption.

Examples:

- "Products in order: [brand A, shade 12] [brand B, shade light] [brand
  C, shade neutral]."
- "Full recipe and the substitutions I tested: [link]."
- "Long-form breakdown of why this works on my channel."

### 4. Second hook into a related video

A teaser that sends the viewer to another piece of your content. Works
best when the original video opens a loop the related video closes.

Examples:

- "If you liked this, the 'why everyone gets retinol wrong' video on my
  profile is the prequel."
- "Part 2 (the part nobody talks about) is the one above this in my
  grid."

## What not to put in the pinned comment

- "Comment YES for the link" or any other bait-shaped trigger. See
  `anti-patterns.md`.
- A repeat of the caption or the on-screen hook. Wasted slot.
- "Thanks for watching, follow for more." Wasted slot, bait-shaped.
- Any link that goes to a bait-and-switch (caption promises a free
  guide, link delivers a $297 course). Triggers spam reports and kills
  trust.

## Self-replying to seed conversation

On all three platforms, posting a real question or hot take from your
own account as the first comment seeds direction for the rest. This
is **not** engagement bait when it's a genuine opinion or a real
question (not "tag a friend below"). It works because the rest of the
section anchors to it.

Examples:

- "Honest question: did I miss the actual answer here, or is everyone
  else missing it too?"
- "I keep going back and forth on this one. Curious what you'd pick."

## The "answer the obvious question" technique

Look at what one comment every viewer is about to leave. The
predictable confusion, the obvious "where do I buy this", the
inevitable "but doesn't X cause Y". Two options:

1. **Pre-empt** in the pinned comment.
2. **Leave space** for it and answer it with a video reply (TikTok) or
   a pinned reply (Instagram, Shorts). This surfaces the comment and
   your reply at the top, and on TikTok the video reply also gets fresh
   distribution.

The second move doubles as a content engine: one video becomes two.

## Comment-to-DM automation, with eyes open

Comment-to-DM via Meta's official API is supported on Instagram (tools
like ManyChat, Spur, Inrō). Practical caps: about 200 automated DMs
per hour, and you can only DM users who engaged in the last 24 hours.

The trade-off: the visible "comment YES for the link" caption that
triggers the automation is exactly the phrase Meta classifies as
engagement bait. You bank automation conversions while paying a
distribution tax on the host post.

Two mitigations:

- Phrase the trigger as a **topical word**, not a yes/no: "comment
  ROUTINE for the steps" reads less bait-shaped than "comment YES".
- **Deliver what you promise** in the DM, immediately, before any
  pitch. Bait-and-switch DM funnels get reported and demoted.

## Timing

- Pin the comment **before** the post goes anywhere. The first 30 to
  60 minutes of a post's life are when the pin works hardest.
- If a comment from another viewer (the perfect objection-killer, a
  great question) shows up later, you can swap it in.
- Edit the pinned comment as the conversation develops. The pin is not
  set-and-forget.
