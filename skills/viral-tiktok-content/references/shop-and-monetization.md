# Shop and monetization

TikTok pays creators through two mostly-separate systems: the Creator
Rewards Program (for organic video reach) and TikTok Shop (for product
sales via affiliate or direct seller relationships). The mechanics, the
content shapes, and the math are different. Pick the one that fits the
goal, then build content to its rules.

## Creator Rewards Program

The successor to the Creator Fund. Pays per qualifying view on videos
that meet the eligibility criteria.

Eligibility (all required):

- **10,000 followers minimum.**
- **100,000 qualifying views in the trailing 30 days.**
- **18 or older.**
- **Account based in US, UK, Germany, France, Japan, or Brazil.**
- **Creator account** (Business accounts are not eligible).
- **Videos over 60 seconds.** Sub-60s clips do not earn under Creator
  Rewards.

The "60 seconds" rule is the binding constraint. A 35-second cut that
ranks brilliantly for reach earns zero from Creator Rewards. If
monetization is the goal, write to the 60s+ shape (see
`script-structure.md`).

RPM (revenue per 1,000 qualifying views) typically lands around
$0.20–$1.00 for standard content, $1–$6+ for finance, tech, and
education niches in US and Western European markets. Creator Rewards
pays multiple times what the legacy Creator Fund paid for equivalent
performance, but the gate (eligibility and 60s+ length) is harder to
clear.

## TikTok Shop

The product-sales surface. The biggest monetization story on the
platform by gross merchandise value. Two paths:

1. **TikTok Shop seller.** You run the storefront, list products,
   handle fulfillment. Direct sales through shoppable video, LIVE
   shopping, and the Shop tab.
2. **TikTok Shop affiliate.** You promote other sellers' products and
   earn commission on the sales you drive. The affiliate channel drives
   roughly 42% of US TikTok Shop GMV; creator-led selling is the
   dominant motion, not brand-direct advertising.

Commission ranges:

| Category    | Typical commission |
|-------------|--------------------|
| Electronics | ~5% |
| Home goods  | ~8–12% |
| Average US  | ~13% |
| Beauty      | ~15–30% |

The average rate is roughly 13% in the US. Beauty pays the highest;
electronics pays the lowest. Pick affiliate products with commission
that matches the work required to demo them well.

## Problem-first content

TikTok's own ads playbook frames the shoppable content shape as
hook → body → close. The hook has to land within six seconds, and ads
that show the product on screen drive roughly 65% higher brand affinity
than those that do not.

The shape that converts:

1. **Hook on the problem, not the product.** "My skin used to peel
   every time I exfoliated." Not "Check out this new serum."
2. **Product on screen by second 6.** Visible, in real use, not a
   static product shot.
3. **Demo the product solving the problem.** Watch it happen. The
   visual is the proof; the voiceover is the context.
4. **Soft close.** The link or product card is the close. The verbal
   CTA can be light because the UI is doing the work.

Direct-response without the problem framing underperforms because the
surface reads ad-shaped and the second-3 cliff fires early.

## The visibility-trust-conversion funnel

Single-video conversion is rare. The content pattern that compounds:

1. **Visibility content.** Wide-reach, problem-first, no product push.
   Builds the audience that will eventually convert.
2. **Trust content.** Founder, behind-the-scenes, demonstration of the
   product in real use (not in studio), customer voices. Builds
   credibility.
3. **Conversion content.** Shoppable video with the soft close. Lands
   harder because the viewer already trusts the source.

Direct-to-conversion without the trust layer typically converts at
1–2% of what a trust-layered funnel does. For new sellers, the trust
content has to come first.

## Shoppable video format

The native shoppable video format on TikTok adds product cards inside
the post. Available to TikTok Shop sellers and to creators in the
affiliate program.

Shape that works:

- **21–34 seconds** for cold reach with a shoppable card.
- **45–60 seconds** for trust-building demo with the card visible
  throughout.
- **Problem in the hook**, product in frame by second 6, real-use demo
  through the middle, soft close at the end.

See `assets/shop-video-template.md` for the fillable skeleton.

## LIVE shopping

A separate format with its own playbook (long-form, host-driven, real-
time Q&A). Out of scope for short-form, but two short-form-relevant
mechanics:

- **LIVE auto-highlights** clip compelling moments into shoppable videos
  that run on FYP after the stream ends. Treat the LIVE as raw material
  for short-form output.
- **Going LIVE drives short-form distribution** in the hours around the
  stream. If you post a short-form piece right before going LIVE, the
  short tends to ride some of the LIVE notification reach.

## The monetization tension

Short for reach, 60s+ for paid views.

- A 21–34s cut produces the highest raw completion rate (~62% average,
  vs ~48% for 60s+). Best for cold reach and follower growth, but earns
  nothing from Creator Rewards directly.
- A 60s+ cut earns Creator Rewards if it performs, but the completion
  math is harder (~40% gate).

For serious accounts, the working pattern is: build the format short
for reach, post the strongest hits as longer cuts for monetization, do
not publish the same content in both lengths inside a few days.

For TikTok Shop sellers, the math changes: the per-sale commission can
dwarf Creator Rewards RPM, so optimize for shoppable conversion shapes
(21–34s with a card) and let monetization come from sales, not RPM.

## Honest framing for clients and brand work

When you sell content services or brand deals on TikTok:

- **Do not promise FYP placement.** No one can. Promise the format and
  the shape; let performance speak.
- **Quote in views and saves, not just views.** Saves are the higher-
  intent signal and read better as a deliverable.
- **Set expectation that test-then-expand means slow start.** A great
  video can take 48–72 hours to break out of the seed cohort. Brands
  that read 24-hour numbers as failure tend to kill content too early.
