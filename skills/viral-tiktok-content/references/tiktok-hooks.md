# TikTok-shaped hooks

The hook is the first 1–3 seconds and the first line of on-screen text. On
TikTok specifically, it carries even more weight than on Reels or Shorts:
the FYP swipes fast, the seed cohort is small, and the second-3 drop is the
cleanest signal the algorithm reads. If the hook fails, nothing else in the
script matters.

The general hook archetypes in `viral-hooks` still apply. This file covers
what shifts when you write for TikTok specifically.

## What changes on TikTok

- **Sound on, often.** Unlike Reels (often watched muted), TikTok viewers
  typically play with sound. The hook works on both tracks: the visual
  carries when muted, the spoken line carries when audible.
- **No runway.** The viewer is already mid-scroll on the previous video.
  You inherit zero attention. The first frame must compete with whatever
  they just left.
- **Format reads as signal.** A "channel-style" intro (logo, "welcome
  back") signals creator-channel format, which the FYP does not prioritize.
  Lead with the content, not the brand.

## TikTok-shaped hook archetypes

These are the patterns that consistently survive the 3-second cliff in
retention analyses. Use a mix when generating a set of 6–10 hooks.

### Middle of action

Open inside the high-stakes moment, not the setup. Camera is already
pouring, applying, lifting, talking to the person, walking out. The viewer
walks in mid-scene and has to stay to figure out what is happening.

Example shape: *"Day 47 of using this $39 thing instead of my $400 setup."*

Works because: the visual is already moving, the line implies a backstory
the viewer wants resolved, and there is no warm-up to swipe through.

### Pattern interrupt / contrarian claim

State something that contradicts what the viewer assumes. Curiosity-gap
hooks measure substantially higher 3-second hold than descriptive hooks.

Example shape: *"Expensive coffee gear is why your coffee tastes worse."*

Works because: the claim creates an open loop the viewer needs closed.
Must be defensible in the payoff, or the save rate craters and the comments
turn.

### Confession framing

Position the video as a secret, an insider take, or something you are
"not supposed to" say. Drives the "I have to hear this" decision before
the algorithm reads the swipe-away.

Example shape: *"I work in coffee. I'm not supposed to like this. I use
it every day."*

Works because: confession reads as social risk taken by the creator, which
the viewer subconsciously reads as honesty.

### Withhold / product-tease

Show the result or effect; hold back the product or method. This is the
dominant save-driving mechanic on TikTok for product content.

Example shape: *"I'm not going to say the brand, but my skin in 4 weeks."*

Works because: viewers stay to find out what it is, and the comments fill
with "what is it?" which feeds the conversation signal. Do not withhold so
long it reads as bait.

### Visual + verbal compound

Striking visual paired with a strong line in the first ~2 seconds. The
visual carries the muted viewers, the line carries the sound-on viewers.

Example shape: slow pour, ice cracking, on-screen text *"the $39 coffee
maker that quietly replaced my whole shelf."*

Works because: it stops both kinds of scroll at once. Particularly strong
for demo and unboxing content.

### Sensory anchor

ASMR, unboxing, unusual texture, novel product or scene in frame. Stops
the scroll via novelty rather than language.

Example shape: hands cracking the foil seal on a product, a satisfying
texture, an unexpected object close-up.

Works because: novelty signal is processed pre-cognitively. The viewer
pauses before they decide to.

### In media res result

Start on the result, then back-fill the story. Strong for before/after,
transformations, and demos.

Example shape: split-screen of "before" already on the right, on-screen
text *"how this $12 thing saved my hair."*

Works because: the payoff is visible immediately, so the curiosity is
"how did this happen" rather than "why should I care."

## Anti-patterns that blow the 3-second cliff

- **Logo or intro animation in frame 1.** Burns 1–2 seconds and signals
  channel-format.
- **"Hey guys, welcome back."** Adds nothing, narrows the audience to
  existing fans on a platform that does not reward fan-channel format.
- **Setup sentence before the payoff.** "Today I'm going to talk about why
  X matters" loses to the actual interesting line.
- **Brand-first.** "Check out our new product" leads with the thing the
  viewer cares least about. Lead with the viewer's outcome.
- **Vague benefit.** "Level up your routine" has no concrete noun, no
  number, no stake. Replace with the specific thing.
- **Cleverness that needs explaining.** A pun the viewer has to decode
  loses to a flat sentence they understand instantly.

## Generating a strong set

When the user asks for TikTok hooks:

- Produce **6–10 across different archetypes**, not 6 rewordings of one.
- **Label each by archetype** so the user learns the pattern.
- **Lead with the payoff or the stake.** Never setup, never brand.
- **Make the first line concrete.** A number, a named thing, a stakes word.
- **Say it out loud.** If the hook takes longer than ~2 seconds to deliver,
  cut it down.
- **Pair the hook with on-screen text.** 3–5 word bold text, paired with
  rapid motion in the opening shot.
