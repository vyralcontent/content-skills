# Script structure by length

Every TikTok script is a chain: each beat has to earn the next second. The
spine is the same across lengths (hook, escalation, payoff, optional CTA),
but the shape changes by tier. Pick the length on purpose, then build to
its math.

For the completion targets per tier, see `retention-math.md`. For hook
archetypes, see `tiktok-hooks.md`.

## The spine

`Hook → Escalation → Payoff → (loop or CTA)`

- **Hook (0–3s):** open a loop, state a stake, or land a visual that stops
  the scroll. Anchor in `tiktok-hooks.md`.
- **Escalation:** raise tension, add specificity, advance the story.
  Connect beats with "but" or "therefore," not "and then." If two beats
  are joined by "and then," the second one probably is not earning its
  place.
- **Payoff:** deliver the hook's promise, visibly. Show the result, not
  just say it.
- **Loop or CTA:** either land the final frame so it reads as the start
  (drives rewatches) or land one specific ask ("save this for next time").
  Not both.

## 21–34 second shape (the sweet spot)

This is the default for cold reach and follower growth. Target completion
is ~70%. One re-hook is enough; the runway is short.

```
0–3s     HOOK              Lead with payoff or stake. Visual + spoken line.
3–8s     ESCALATION 1      Add specificity. The number, the named thing.
8–18s    ESCALATION 2      Raise the stakes or reveal context.
                           Insert one re-hook around second 12 if energy plateaus.
18–28s   PAYOFF            Show the result. Visible, concrete.
28–34s   LOOP / CTA        Final frame loops to the opening, or one clear ask.
```

Cut shot or angle every 2–3 seconds. No dead air at the start or end.

## 60-second shape (Creator Rewards eligible)

Target completion drops to ~50%, the runway is twice as long, and the
algorithm reads two retention resets as the difference between "people
finished it" and "people bailed at second 35."

```
0–3s     HOOK              Same rules as the short shape. No softening.
3–10s    PROMISE STACK     Tease the payoff explicitly. "Here is what you'll see."
10–25s   ESCALATION 1      First substantive beat. Open the first loop.
25–28s   RE-HOOK           "But here is the part nobody expects." Visual reset.
28–45s   ESCALATION 2      Second substantive beat. Close the first loop.
45–52s   PAYOFF            Show the result. The strongest visual lives here.
52–60s   LOOP              Land the final frame so it reads as the opening.
```

If you cannot honestly fill 60 seconds with substance, do not stretch a
shorter cut. Pad-stretched videos lose on completion rate.

## 90s+ shape (two retention resets, payoff stack)

For story-driven content and deep-dive demos. Target ~40% completion.
Three retention resets, two payoff beats, a clear arc.

```
0–3s     HOOK              Promise the payoff explicitly.
3–15s    SETUP             Set the stakes. One open loop.
15–18s   RE-HOOK 1         "What happens next is why I made this video."
18–45s   ACT 1             First substantive beat. Close one loop, open a new one.
45–50s   RE-HOOK 2         Visual reset. New angle, new pace.
50–75s   ACT 2             Second substantive beat. Build to the payoff.
75–85s   PAYOFF 1          Show the first result.
85–95s   PAYOFF 2          Show the second result, or the implication.
95s+     LOOP / CTA        Loop the final frame or land one specific ask.
```

Long-form on TikTok lives or dies on the re-hooks. Two of them, minimum.

## Pacing across all lengths

- **Cut every 2–3 seconds.** Shot, angle, framing, or on-screen text. The
  eye should never settle into "I have seen enough."
- **Front-load value.** Survive the steep early drop-off by putting your
  second-strongest moment near the top. Save the strongest for the payoff.
- **Pattern reset mid-video.** Switch audio genre or cut tempo to reset
  attention. Particularly effective at the 25–30% mark of any length.
- **No dead air at start or end.** Tighten to the bone. The first and last
  lines are usually the most over-written; cut them last.

## Loop techniques

The cheapest way to drive rewatches:

- **Final frame matches the opening visual.** Same angle, same prop in
  frame, same lighting.
- **Final line resolves into the opening line.** "And that is why I
  said…" cuts to the opening line.
- **Question loops to answer.** The hook asks; the final frame shows the
  setup that produces the answer, prompting a rewatch to catch the middle.

A clean loop drives completion above 100% on sub-15s clips, which is the
only way to clear that tier's gate.

## Save-bait patterns

If saves are the goal (most product, educational, or utility content):

- **List of 3 or 5.** Containers the viewer can come back to.
- **"Send this to a friend who…"** Specific viewer call-out.
- **"Save this for next time you…"** Future-intent framing.
- **Concrete payoff visible at end.** The viewer needs the result in frame
  to know what they are saving for.

## Dual narrative

Run two stories at once: voiceover says one thing, visuals show a
different but complementary thing. Eyes and ears each get fed, attention
deepens. Keep the two complementary, never contradictory.

Example: voiceover narrates the lesson, visuals show the demo. Or the
voiceover tells the story, the on-screen text lists the key facts.

## Critique mode (improving a draft)

When the user hands you a script to improve:

1. **Time it out loud.** Length tier sets the bar. Mark the second the
   hook lands and the second the payoff arrives.
2. **Find the second-3 cliff.** Read the first three seconds. Does the
   hook deliver, or is there setup?
3. **Find the sag.** Mark the exact line where the energy plateaus. That
   is where the re-hook goes.
4. **Check the loop or payoff.** Sub-15s, does the final frame loop?
   Over 30s, is the result visible, not just stated?
5. **Quote the weak lines.** Do not say "tighten the intro." Say "cut
   lines 1–2, open on line 3, it is your real hook."
