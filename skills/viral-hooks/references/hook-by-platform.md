# Hooks by platform

The archetype is portable. The execution isn't. TikTok, Reels, and
Shorts each reward slightly different pacing, text style, and
tolerance for setup inside the hook window. For deeper algorithm
mechanics, see the platform-specific skills (`viral-tiktok-content`,
`viral-instagram-reels`, `viral-youtube-shorts`). This file is the
hook-window cheat sheet.

## TikTok

The hook window is roughly 1.5 to 3 seconds. The algorithm decides
fast. Most underperforming TikToks fail in the first three seconds,
not later.

What works:

- **Authority + curiosity + payoff promise, resolved by second 3.**
  Credibility in second one ("I've spent six years in [field]"),
  curiosity gap in second two ("the real reason isn't [common
  answer]"), payoff promise in second three ("it's actually [hint]").
  All three resolve before the algorithm finishes the early read.
- **Product or outcome showcase, lead with the finished thing.**
  Outperforms storytelling openers by a meaningful margin. Show the
  result first, explain after.
- **Specific (often odd) numbers.** "$413 burned" reads more measured
  than "$400."
- **Contrarian opens.** The brain struggles to leave a contradiction
  unresolved.

What underperforms:

- Story openers ("let me tell you about the time…").
- "Hey guys, today we're going to talk about…"
- Vague teases ("you won't believe what happened").
- Anything that takes more than 3 seconds to resolve.

On-screen text style:

- Three to seven words. High contrast. Center or upper-third.
- TikTok's UI sits on the bottom and right. Keep text out of those
  zones.
- The native font reads as native. Custom fonts can read as ad.

Sound:

- Trending sound, when it fits, signals freshness. When it doesn't,
  it's noise.
- Original audio is fine for talking-head; the spoken hook is
  indexed for search, so the verbal line is also discovery copy.

## Instagram Reels

The hook window is tight. A large share of viewers drop in the first
three seconds. The algorithm reads early dropoff as a signal that the
video isn't worth promoting further.

What works:

- **Visual-led hooks.** Reels rewards strong first-frame composition
  more than TikTok does. A muted, paused-thumb viewer should get the
  hook from the frame alone.
- **A 60% or higher 3-second hold rate.** That's the benchmark a
  strong hook tends to clear. Above 70% tends to unlock broader
  distribution to non-followers.
- **Tight on-screen text in the first frame.** Reels viewers often
  scroll fast; the text earns the pause before the voice lands.
- **Carousel-style "first slide is the hook" thinking.** Reels lives
  in a feed that's heavy on static visuals, so the hook frame should
  read like a strong feed post.

What underperforms:

- Same anti-patterns as TikTok, plus:
- Recycled TikTok exports with the TikTok watermark. Reels' ranking
  appears to demote them.
- Long captions in the first frame. Reels readers don't read past
  the first line.

On-screen text style:

- Bigger and bolder than TikTok. The Reels UI is less crowded but the
  feed competes with high-production carousels.
- Keep the text out of the right-side action rail.

Pacing:

- Slightly slower cuts than TikTok work because Reels viewers
  tolerate a half-beat more setup. Slightly. The hook still has to
  resolve fast.

## YouTube Shorts

The Shorts feed lives next to the Suggested column and the home feed,
so the hook is competing with both short and long content. The
top-of-funnel metric is Viewed vs Swiped Away (VVSA). A 70 to 90%
VVSA tends to break out. Under 60% tends to flop.

What works:

- **Click-to-unpause framing.** The first frame depicts a scene mid-
  action that the viewer instinctively wants to play to see what
  happens next. The opening frame is designed to look paused.
- **A first sentence engineered to echo the title.** The title and
  the first line have to agree. If they don't, satisfaction signals
  drop and the Shorts feed stops distributing.
- **No subscribe CTA in the hook.** The subscribe button sits at the
  bottom of the Shorts player and accidental taps are common. The
  right early CTA is "watch the full video on my channel," not
  "subscribe."

What underperforms:

- Welcome-back intros and logo animations. The Shorts feed punishes
  delay in hook delivery harder than TikTok or Reels.
- Channel jingles in the first second.
- Misleading hooks. Satisfaction-weighted discovery means a hook
  that gets the click but tanks satisfaction is now demoted more
  than ever.

On-screen text style:

- YouTube viewers read text more readily than TikTok viewers (the
  audience overlaps with long-form). Slightly longer captions are
  tolerated, but three to seven words is still the sweet spot.
- The Shorts UI sits on the right with engagement buttons and on
  the bottom with the title. Keep text out of those zones.

Pacing:

- Faster than YouTube long-form, similar to TikTok. The Shorts feed
  doesn't wait for a hook to warm up.

## Long-form YouTube (for comparison)

The cold open serves a different job: validate the click, reduce
time-to-value, and signal that the rest of the video will pay off.
The practical window is 15 to 30 seconds, longer than short-form,
because the viewer has already committed by clicking.

What works:

- A cold open that shows the most arresting moment of the video
  before the title card.
- A first-minute retention curve that escalates rather than ramps.
- The MrBeast-style "minute 0-1 maximum hook intensity, then
  re-engagement spike at minute 3, then payoff" shape.

What underperforms:

- Sponsor reads in the first 7 seconds.
- Logo intros and channel jingles.
- Long autobiographical setups.

The hook on long-form is a system, not a single line. The thumbnail
and title do the pre-click work. The cold open does the post-click
work. Both are part of the same hook system.

## Paid social ads

Cold audiences punish anything that smells like a pitch in the first
two seconds.

What works:

- **Direct audience callouts.** "Attention freelancers." "If you
  paid more than $50 for a serum, watch this."
- **Problem / agitation / solution (PAS).** Name the pain, sharpen
  it, then offer the fix.
- **Confessional opens.** "I burned $400 testing this so you don't
  have to."
- **Bold-claim opens.** "This is the only [product] that does
  [outcome]." Only if defensible.

What underperforms:

- Direct-to-purchase hooks. Cold audiences need warming.
- Brand-first openers.
- Anything that sounds like an ad in the first second.

The hook on a paid social ad is the offer in miniature: specific,
benefit-led, matched to the funnel position.

## Cross-platform repurposing

When taking a hook from one platform to another:

- **Keep the archetype.** A Contrarian hook stays Contrarian.
- **Swap the pacing.** TikTok cuts faster than Reels which cuts
  faster than long-form.
- **Swap the text style.** TikTok native font; Reels bigger and
  bolder; Shorts slightly longer tolerated.
- **Swap the audio.** Trending sound on one platform doesn't
  necessarily trend on another.
- **Re-shoot the first frame for each platform's UI.** Text
  placement is platform-specific.

The wrong move is shipping the same export with the same watermark
across three platforms. The right move is keeping the angle and
re-cutting the execution.
