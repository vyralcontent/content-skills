# Reels hook craft

Hook archetypes work the same across short-form. The Reels-specific work
happens in three places: the three-second cliff that decides whether the seed
test expands, the sound-off viewer who needs the hook in text on the first
frame, and the payoff promise that has to read as shareable from the start.

For archetypes themselves (curiosity gap, withhold, stakes, bold claim, named
viewer, mistake confession, listicle, in media res), see the `viral-hooks`
skill. This file is what changes when the surface is Reels.

## The three-second cliff

Reels has an explicit public hurdle at the three-second mark. Drop-off before
three seconds reads as a failed hook and caps the seed test before the
algorithm can expand the audience. Everything in the opening is in service of
keeping the viewer past that cliff.

Practical implications:

- The first frame has to do real work. No logo intro, no slow zoom-in, no
  black frame, no "hi guys."
- The first spoken word should be the most interesting word in the line. Cut
  any preamble before it.
- Front-load the visual hook. Movement, a face, a reveal, a contrast. A flat
  static frame in the first second is a flat-line in retention.
- Length doesn't excuse a slow open. A 30-second Reel and a 90-second Reel
  both die at the same three-second cliff if the open is weak.

## Sound-off legibility

A meaningful share of Reels viewers watch with the phone muted, especially
during the early seed test. If the hook is only spoken and not on screen, the
sound-off viewer scrolls. That's a failed seed signal you didn't need to
spend.

Rules for the on-screen hook:

- Three to five words, bold, on the first frame. Not a sentence. A headline.
- Restate the hook, don't transcribe the whole voiceover. The text should
  carry the meaning even with no audio.
- Place it where Instagram's UI won't cover it. Keep text out of the bottom
  fifth and top fifth where captions and account info sit.
- Use high-contrast styling. The default white sans-serif on busy footage
  fails on a phone in sunlight.

The same hook line you'd say works as the on-screen text most of the time.
Tighten it to a headline. Spoken: "If you keep breaking out after every new
serum, this is for you." On screen: "Breakouts from every new serum?"

## Payoff preview

The hook is also a promise. On Reels, the share metric punishes hooks that
make a promise the body never pays off, because viewers who finished feeling
cheated don't send the Reel to anyone. That's a direct hit to sends per reach,
which is the metric that buys cold reach.

A good Reels hook signals shareable curiosity, not generic curiosity. The
viewer should think "I know one specific person who needs to see this" before
the payoff even arrives. Pair the archetype with a named-persona angle so the
share intent forms in the first three seconds.

Spoken hook: "Every dermatologist I asked told me the same thing about niacinamide."
Implied share trigger: "I should send this to the friend who tried niacinamide
last month."

## First-frame composition

The first frame is the thumbnail in the Reels tab, the cover on the grid (if
shared), and the visual that decides whether a thumb keeps scrolling. Treat
it as a hook on its own.

- Lead with a face, a reveal, or a sharp visual contrast. A talking head with
  a clear expression beats a wide product shot.
- Avoid third-party app UI in the frame. CapCut overlays, TikTok timers, and
  app chrome read as reposted content and tank cold distribution (see
  `originality-and-audio.md`).
- The aspect ratio matters for grid framing too. Reels appear on the profile
  grid in a 3:4 crop. A composition centred for 9:16 video can crop badly on
  the grid. Frame the hook in the upper two-thirds.

## On-screen text timing

Match the on-screen text to the spoken beats, but don't transcribe.

- Hook text appears on the first frame and holds for two to three seconds.
- Escalation beats get short text labels, three or four words each.
- The payoff line gets its own bold beat, ideally the cleanest piece of text
  in the video.
- The send-prompt CTA at the end gets its own frame and its own text. Don't
  pile it on top of the payoff.

If on-screen text is constant noise that changes every half-second, the eye
gives up reading and engagement drops. Use text as punctuation, not as a
caption track.

## Anti-patterns

- **"Hey guys, welcome back to my channel."** The single fastest way to fail
  the three-second cliff. Cut everything before the first interesting word.
- **Logo-first intros.** Branded animations and bumper cards burn the cliff
  on someone else's voice (yours, but yesterday).
- **Vague benefit hooks.** "Level up your skincare." The hook has to name a
  concrete result, a specific viewer, or a real stake. See `viral-hooks` for
  archetype options.
- **Hook that only works with audio.** If a sound-off viewer can't tell what
  the Reel is about in two seconds, you've spent your reach on people who
  scrolled.
- **Clickbait without payoff.** A withheld reveal that never reveals tanks
  sends and trains the algorithm that your account makes empty promises.
- **Trying to hook three different audiences at once.** A muddy hook to a
  broad audience reads as nothing to anyone. Pick one viewer per Reel.
