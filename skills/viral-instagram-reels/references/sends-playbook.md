# Sends playbook

A DM share carries roughly three to five times the weight of a like when
Instagram decides whether to push a Reel to non-followers. That makes sends
per reach the single most important metric on Reels for cold growth.
Likes per reach still helps you re-reach existing followers in main feed, but
it does not buy you new audience the way a send does.

The whole point of this playbook is to build the Reel so the natural reaction
is "I should send this to [specific person]." That reaction has to start
forming in the hook, not in the CTA.

## Why sends carry the most weight

A share is a costly action. Liking a Reel takes a half-second tap. Sending a
Reel takes a real decision: who needs this, why, and is it worth the social
cost of the send. Instagram treats that cost as a quality signal because it's
hard to fake. A Reel that earns sends has earned an endorsement from one
person to another, which is what the recommendation system is trying to
predict at scale.

This is also why send-prompts work where like-prompts don't. "Like this if
you agree" is a free action and the algorithm pattern-matches the CTA as
engagement bait. "Send this to the friend who buys every serum" is a
specific instruction that triggers a specific behaviour for a specific
person.

## Structures that earn sends

These are the recurring shapes of high-send Reels. Pick one and build the
script around it. Don't stack three.

1. **Named-persona favour.** The payoff is information that a specific
   recognisable person in the viewer's life needs. The Reel is the favour.
   Example: "If your friend keeps mixing retinol and vitamin C, send this
   before they wreck their skin barrier." The viewer sends because they
   actually know that friend.

2. **Receipt for a running argument.** The Reel ends an ongoing disagreement
   between the viewer and someone they know. Example: a Reel proving a
   common skincare claim is wrong becomes ammunition in a back-and-forth.
   The viewer sends because they win the argument by sending.

3. **Tagged callout.** The hook directly names a behaviour the viewer
   recognises in someone else. Example: "POV: your friend who has tried
   every new mascara." The viewer sends because the Reel is the callout
   they wanted to make themselves.

4. **Useful list with a memorable name.** A Reel with a clear takeaway and a
   name viewers can repeat ("the three-product face") earns sends because
   the viewer thinks "I want to share this list with [person] later." The
   name does the heavy lifting in the DM thread.

5. **Validation send.** A Reel that confirms a feeling the viewer's friend
   has been having. Example: "If your skincare obsessed friend feels guilty
   about throwing out half-used serums, this is the permission slip." Sends
   land as "see, you're not alone."

6. **Concrete how-to for a specific situation.** A Reel that solves a small
   specific problem someone the viewer knows actually has. Generic how-tos
   don't earn sends. Specific how-tos for a recognisable person do.

## Reframing the CTA

A strong CTA names the recipient as well as the action. Compare:

- Weak: "Follow for more." (generic, low value, engagement-bait pattern)
- Weak: "Save this." (better, but no targeted recipient)
- Weak: "Share with a friend." (no friend in particular comes to mind)
- Better: "Send this to the friend who buys every new serum."
- Better: "Send this to your sister who keeps trying retinol on broken skin."
- Better: "Send this to whoever swears by their 12-step routine."

The pattern is: send this to [the specific person who fits this Reel].
Specificity is the lever. A vague "tag a friend" might earn one in twenty
sends. A named persona earns a send from anyone who can picture that person.

Place the CTA at the end of the Reel, in voice and in on-screen text, with
its own beat. Don't bury it under the payoff visual.

## Send-suppressing anti-patterns

- **Generic CTAs.** "Like and follow," "double-tap if you agree," "drop a
  heart." These are engagement-bait patterns and they spend the closing
  seconds on a low-weight action.
- **CTA that asks for nothing in particular.** "What do you think?" earns
  comments at best, no sends.
- **Payoff that solves a problem nobody specific has.** If you can't name
  the person who needs this Reel, the viewer can't either.
- **Educational Reels with no shareable container.** Three good tips with
  no organising name don't get sent. Three tips called "the three-product
  face" do.
- **Hook that promises a payoff the body never delivers.** Viewers who
  feel cheated don't send. That single failure compounds across the Reel's
  whole life.
- **Closing on the creator instead of on the share.** "Thanks for watching,
  see you next time" replaces the most valuable moment with throat-clearing.

## Diagnosing low sends in Insights

If share rate or sends per reach is below your normal baseline:

1. Check the CTA. Was it a named-persona send-prompt, or a generic ask?
2. Check the payoff. Is it useful to a specific recognisable person, or to
   a vague "anyone interested in this"?
3. Check the hook. Did it signal a shareable payoff in the first three
   seconds, or just curiosity for curiosity's sake?
4. Check the topic legibility. Is the niche clear without audio?
   (See `caption-and-search.md`.)

Most low-send Reels fail at the named-persona test, not at the production
quality test. Recut the CTA and the hook before reshooting.

## Working with the rest of the skill

The sends playbook depends on the hook making a shareable promise
(`reels-hook.md`), the body delivering on it (see retention in
`viral-short-form`), and the caption reinforcing the named-persona angle
(`caption-and-search.md`). Sends are the metric, but they're earned by the
whole script. The CTA alone doesn't carry them.
