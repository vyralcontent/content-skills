# Caption and search

Hashtags don't move reach on Reels. Mosseri said so directly. Captions and
on-screen text now do the work hashtags used to: they tell Instagram what
the Reel is about so it can route the Reel to the right Reels-tab viewers,
and they get the Reel indexed in in-app search and on Google.

The caption is also the most underused CTA surface on the platform. It's the
one place where you can write a real send-prompt without burning seconds of
the Reel.

## Captions as SEO

Instagram indexes captions, alt text, and on-screen text for in-app search.
Public posts are also indexed by Google and Bing, which means your Reels can
show up for keyword searches outside Instagram entirely. That changes how a
caption should be written.

Practical rules:

- **First line is the keyword phrase.** Lead with the exact words a person
  would type if they were searching for this Reel. "Niacinamide for oily
  skin" beats "the one product changing my mornings."
- **Write in plain search-friendly language.** Avoid metaphor and inside
  jokes in the first line. Save the voice for later in the caption.
- **One topic per caption.** A caption that hedges across three topics
  trains the algorithm that the account is about all three weakly. Pick the
  one and own it.
- **Use the keyword phrase verbatim once or twice, not five times.** Keyword
  stuffing reads as low quality and is easy to spot.
- **150 to 300 characters is the working sweet spot.** Long enough to load
  the topic, short enough that viewers actually read it. Reels captions that
  are walls of text get ignored.

## On-screen text as a second search signal

Instagram reads on-screen text and adds it to the topic understanding of the
Reel. A Reel with the keyword phrase on the first frame is twice tagged: once
for retention (sound-off viewers see what it's about) and once for indexing
(the platform knows what the Reel is about).

For dual-duty on-screen text:

- The first-frame text should be the keyword phrase in a headline form, not
  a complete sentence. "Breakouts after every new serum?" beats "Today I'm
  going to talk about why you break out after every new serum."
- Use the same vocabulary on screen as in the caption. Consistency
  reinforces the topic signal.
- Don't try to caption the whole voiceover on screen. Punctuation, not
  transcription. (See `reels-hook.md` for on-screen text timing.)

## Hashtags: three to five, no more

The old advice was 30 hashtags stuffed in the first comment. That advice is
dead. Mosseri stated directly that hashtags don't improve reach. They still
act as labels for in-app search and topic categorisation. So use them, but
don't expect them to do anything they don't do.

Working rules:

- **Three to five hashtags, maximum.** More than five reads as engagement
  bait and signals low-quality content.
- **Highly relevant only.** A hashtag that doesn't accurately describe the
  Reel is noise. It also confuses the topic signal.
- **Mix one broad and two to four niche.** One broad (#skincare) for general
  surface area, two to four niche (#niacinamideroutine, #oilyskincare) for
  specific match.
- **In the caption, not in a first comment.** First-comment hashtags are a
  relic of the old algorithm. Put them at the end of the caption.
- **Don't reuse the exact same hashtag set on every post.** Instagram reads
  identical hashtag stacks as automated behaviour.

## Caption structure for Reels

A working caption shape for cold-reach Reels:

```
[Keyword phrase as headline, sentence-case]

[One to two lines that expand the hook and reinforce the topic]

[Send-prompt CTA naming the recipient]

[3 to 5 relevant hashtags]
```

Example skeleton (not a literal template):

```
Niacinamide for oily skin: what the routine actually looks like.

The trick most people miss is layering order. Get it wrong and the
breakouts come back faster than the serum can fix them.

Send this to the friend who buys every new serum.

#skincare #niacinamide #oilyskincare #skincareroutine
```

The caption restates the topic in search-friendly language, expands the
hook into context, drives a send to a named persona, and labels the Reel
with relevant hashtags. It's a complete unit that works with the Reel, not
a throwaway under it.

## Your Algorithm and topic legibility

Instagram now exposes the topic labels it has inferred about a user, and
lets them edit those labels. That cuts both ways for creators.

On the inbound side, your Reel is competing for placement against other
Reels for the same topic labels. If Instagram can't confidently assign a
topic to your Reel, it doesn't know who to show it to and reach suffers.

On the outbound side, the topics you build through your captions, on-screen
text, and engaged audience determine which topic labels your account gets
associated with. Inconsistent topic positioning across Reels makes the
account fuzzy and weakens recommendation.

Practical implication: be ruthlessly consistent on topic across multiple
Reels. Don't post a skincare Reel, a finance Reel, and a travel Reel in the
same week and expect any of them to compound. The clearer your account's
topic signature, the cleaner the recommendation surface for every Reel.

## Profile and grid placement

When you publish a Reel to the grid (an opt-in checkbox), it appears in the
profile grid at a 3:4 aspect ratio. A composition framed for 9:16 video
often crops awkwardly on the grid. If the Reel matters for your profile
brand, frame the hook subject in the upper two-thirds so the grid crop
keeps it.

You can pin Reels to the profile grid (maximum three pins across all
posts), but you cannot reorder or pin within the Reels tab. The Reels tab
itself is strictly reverse-chronological.

## Anti-patterns

- **Hashtag-stuffed captions** ("#fyp #foryou #explore" and 25 more). Reads
  as low-effort, possibly bait, and dilutes the topic signal.
- **Throwaway captions** ("link in bio," a single emoji, "thoughts?"). The
  caption is the SEO surface and the CTA surface. Use it.
- **Captions that contradict the Reel.** A caption that promises one thing
  and a Reel that delivers another confuses topic and tanks trust.
- **Different keyword phrases on screen and in caption.** Pick one and use
  it in both places.
- **Captions written for followers when the Reel is for cold reach.** Most
  Reels viewers don't know you. Write the caption for them.

## Quick checklist before publishing

- First caption line is the keyword phrase a stranger would search.
- On-screen text on the first frame matches the caption's keyword phrase.
- Caption is 150 to 300 characters with a clear send-prompt CTA.
- Three to five highly relevant hashtags, in the caption, at the end.
- Topic of this Reel is consistent with what the account usually posts.
- If the Reel is grid-bound, the hook subject is composed for the 3:4 crop.
