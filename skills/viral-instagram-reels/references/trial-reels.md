# Trial Reels

Trial Reels are Instagram's built-in way to test a Reel on non-followers before
it ever touches your follower feed or your grid. If the goal of the Reel is
cold reach (the usual goal), this is the default first move. A flop costs you
nothing. A winner gives you a confirmed cold-audience signal before you publish
to everyone.

## When to use one (and when to skip)

Default to Trial Reels when all of the following are true:

- The Reel is meant to reach non-followers (most Reels).
- The account is public and has at least about 1,000 followers (eligibility).
- You're not certain the hook lands. Trials are cheapest exactly when you're
  least sure.
- You want a clean test on a new format, hook style, topic, or length.

Skip Trial Reels and publish directly when:

- The Reel is primarily for your existing followers (a behind-the-scenes, a
  thank-you, a community-only joke). Followers don't see trials.
- The Reel is time-sensitive in a way that needs to hit the grid right now (a
  product drop, a live event, a calendar moment).
- You're under the 1,000-follower threshold. The toggle isn't available yet.
  Focus on publishing and on the 0 to 1K slog.

## How the mechanic actually works

- Toggle "Trial" before you publish. The Reel goes out only to non-followers.
- Your followers see nothing in feed, nothing in the Reels tab, nothing on
  your grid. The Reel is invisible to people who already know you.
- At roughly the 24-hour mark you get a read: views, likes, comments, shares,
  plus a comparison to your previous trials. This is the cold-audience verdict.
- At roughly the 72-hour mark Instagram may auto-share the Reel to your
  followers if it judges performance strong. You can also manually publish to
  everyone at any point, including immediately.
- If you do nothing and Instagram doesn't auto-share, the Reel stays a trial.

## What a trial actually confirms

A trial is a non-follower test. The signal it gives you is "did this Reel earn
distribution from people who don't know you." That's the signal that maps to
cold reach on the public Reel.

A trial does not tell you:

- Whether your existing followers would have engaged. That's a different
  audience with different expectations.
- Whether the Reel would have looked good on your grid. The grid is its own
  composition problem (see `caption-and-search.md` for thumbnail notes).
- Whether the comments would have looked the same with followers in the mix.
  Follower comments often anchor the early thread.

Treat a trial as a hook-and-topic test for cold audiences. If it converts,
publishing is a low-risk decision. If it doesn't, the hook or the topic was the
wrong bet for non-followers, not necessarily a failure of the whole idea.

## Reading the 24-hour numbers

Compare against your own prior trials, not against your follower-feed posts.
The audience is different and so the baseline is different.

- **Views trending higher than your usual trials**: the seed test expanded.
  The hook held the first three seconds well enough.
- **Sends and saves higher than usual**: the payoff is shareable and the topic
  is legible. This is the strongest "publish to everyone" signal.
- **High views, low sends and follows**: the Reel got reach but didn't make
  the topic legible. People watched, scrolled, didn't think to share or follow.
  Rewrite the on-screen text and CTA before re-trying.
- **Low views even on the trial**: the hook failed the three-second cliff or
  the topic is too narrow for cold audiences. Reshoot the first three seconds.

See `insights.md` for the full metric playbook. The trial dashboard is a
subset of those numbers, scoped to non-followers.

## The publish decision

You have three real options at the 24 to 72 hour mark:

1. **Share to everyone now.** Numbers are clearly above your trial baseline.
   Don't wait. Sustained early velocity matters and a published Reel can keep
   compounding.
2. **Let Instagram auto-share at 72 hours.** A passive yes. Useful when the
   numbers are okay but not obvious, and you want Instagram's own judgement
   on whether to push it.
3. **Leave it as a trial.** The numbers underperformed. Don't publish. Learn
   from the trial and recut, or move on. Nothing of this Reel will count
   against your follower-feed metrics or your grid.

## Anti-patterns

- Trialing only winners you already love. Trial Reels are most valuable on the
  drafts you're least sure of. If you only trial the safe ones, you learn
  nothing.
- Treating a trial flop as a verdict on the topic forever. Often it's the hook
  or the first frame. Recut the opening three seconds and re-trial before
  abandoning the idea.
- Trialing too rarely. The dashboard's "compare to prior trials" view gets
  more useful with a meaningful sample. Run trials regularly on your weaker
  drafts and the baseline starts to mean something.
- Trialing a Reel and then publishing the same cut at the wrong time.
  Auto-share decisions and your own re-publishes both restart the clock. Pick
  a publish window with intent.
