# Anti-patterns

The failure modes specific to ideation. Each one is common, each one is
diagnosable, and each one has a structural fix. When the user describes
a symptom that matches, name the anti-pattern and prescribe the fix.

## Brainstorming without constraints

**Symptom.** "I sat down to think of videos and got nothing" or "I
have a list of 50 ideas and they're all generic."

**Why it fails.** The brain free-associates instead of triangulating.
Without a constraint, every option is equally weighted, which means
nothing rises. Every framework in `frameworks.md` is a way of
injecting constraint so the brain has something to push against.

**Fix.** Never ideate without a constraint. Pick a pillar, a format, an
audience, or a problem. Then ideate inside that box. Volume comes from
moving across multiple boxes (the content matrix), not from making the
box bigger.

## Copying without re-angling

**Symptom.** The user has identified a competitor outlier and wants to
make their version of it.

**Why it fails.** Studying competitor outliers is a research input, not
a production output. A frame-for-frame copy of a successful video tends
to underperform because the original's reach was partly novelty in its
niche. The mechanic transferred; the surface didn't.

**Fix.** Galloway's rule. Extract the mechanic (comparison,
transformation, deadline, cost confession, before/after) and
transplant it. Don't copy the surface. A "comparison" video on coffee
gear and a "comparison" video on programming languages share the
mechanic but produce completely different videos.

## The idea-wall problem (capture without execution)

**Symptom.** A growing backlog of captured ideas that never become
videos. The user feels productive but their publish cadence is flat.

**Why it fails.** Capturing ideas is free; producing videos is
expensive. Without triage and a deletion rule, the backlog grows
faster than it can be worked through. Eventually the inbox is
overwhelming and the creator stops looking at it.

**Fix.** Three rules:

1. **Status on every idea.** Inbox, Triage, Scripted, Filmed,
   Published. An idea without a status is invisible.
2. **Deletion rule.** Any idea older than ~90 days that hasn't been
   touched gets archived or killed. Pretending it's still active
   clogs the pipeline.
3. **Distill or die.** Capture without distillation is inert (see
   `idea-evaluation.md`). A captured note like "morning routines" is
   a topic. A distilled idea has a hook, payoff, format, and length
   target.

`assets/idea-tracker.md` is the minimum-viable pipeline.

## Niching too narrow

**Symptom.** Idea drought after roughly 3 months. Declining engagement
across multiple videos at once (not just one). Burnout despite trying
different angles.

**Why it fails.** The niche can't sustain a year of weekly output.
Test: can the user brainstorm 50 ideas inside it in 20 minutes? If
not, the niche is too tight.

**Fix.** Widen one pillar at a time, driven by audience signal. Read
comments and DMs from the last month. Asks that recur but don't fit
any current pillar are the candidates. Add one. Don't blow up the
niche. See `pillars.md`.

## Niching too broad (or not at all)

**Symptom.** Engagement is flat across every post regardless of
quality. The account is "interesting" but not memorable. The
algorithm can't decide who to serve it to.

**Why it fails.** No audience expectation, no compounding, no clarity.
Each video starts from zero.

**Fix.** Pick the two highest-performing posts of the last 90 days.
What do they share? That's the real pillar the account has been
working in by accident. Lean into it. Cut the rest or move it to a
side account.

A subset case: Dan Koe's "anti-niche" position argues against pillars
entirely. It can work, but only with a strong premise (Jay Clouse's
spiky point of view) that does the work pillars would otherwise do.
For most creators, 3 to 5 pillars under one umbrella identity is the
safer middle.

## Inspiration over system

**Symptom.** The user produces in bursts and then goes silent for
weeks. Output is high quality when it happens and inconsistent
overall.

**Why it fails.** Inspiration is not a planning input. Every prolific
creator runs a system; none of them rely on inspiration. Waiting for a
good idea to strike tends to be the largest single source of
content-creation burnout.

**Fix.** Treat ideation as a scheduled production stage with inputs
and outputs, not a creative practice. The mining loop runs on a
schedule. The brainstorm runs on a schedule. The triage runs on a
schedule. The discipline is in the calendar, not in the moment.

The hidden benefit: flat output produces enough samples to learn what
works. Burst output doesn't. The system wins on volume, but more
importantly it wins on data.

## Producing before testing (skipping the cheap-test step)

**Symptom.** The user spent two days producing a polished short of an
idea that flopped, and is now demoralized.

**Why it fails.** A polished 30-second short is roughly 10x more
expensive in time than tweeting the idea first and seeing if anyone
reacts. Producing an unvalidated idea inverts the cost-test ratio.

**Fix.** Hormozi's ladder. Ideas earn their way up the production cost
ladder. A tweet or single-line post is the cheapest test. A polished
short is the next tier. Long-form is the top. Only proven ideas earn
the spend. See `repurposing.md` for the full version of this engine.

For ideas that can't be cheap-tested (a complex narrative, a
production-heavy concept), the substitute is the hook-conception test
from `idea-evaluation.md`. If you can't write a sharp hook for it,
don't produce it.

## Treating the hook as an afterthought

**Symptom.** The user has a fully scripted video and is now thinking
about how to open it.

**Why it fails.** The hook isn't an ornament on a finished script; it
is the screening filter for whether the idea was worth making. By
writing the script before the hook, the creator has already committed
production effort to an idea whose viability is unknown.

**Fix.** Write the hook first, always. If the hook is strong, build
the script to deliver it. If you can't write a strong hook, kill the
idea before the script. See `idea-evaluation.md` for the full
hook-conception test and the `viral-hooks` skill for the hook writing
itself.

## Over-tooling (productivity theater)

**Symptom.** The user has a Notion database with 47 fields, three
swipe files, two AI prompt libraries, and a custom GPT, but hasn't
published in 3 weeks.

**Why it fails.** The ratio of saved swipe content / Notion templates /
GPT prompts to actual published videos is the cleanest single signal
of whether a creator is in production mode or in productivity-theater
mode.

**Fix.** Strip the toolkit. Apple Notes for fast capture, one Notion
database with five fields max for triage, the rest goes. Add a tool
back only after the existing stack has produced 30+ videos. Tooling
sophistication tends to be inversely correlated with output.

## Mixing capture and creation modes

**Symptom.** The user opens their notes app to capture a new idea and
ends up spending an hour trying to develop it into a script. The
capture queue grows; nothing ships.

**Why it fails.** Capture is fast and low-judgement. Creation is slow
and high-judgement. They use different mental muscles. Trying to do
both at the same time kills both: capture slows down because every
note feels like it should be developed; creation fragments because
each session is interrupted by new captures.

**Fix.** Two-mode rule. Fast capture goes straight to the inbox, no
development. Deeper processing happens in scheduled batches. The
handoff between the two is the part to design carefully; the modes
themselves stay separate. See `mining.md`.

## Building a series without testing the premise

**Symptom.** The user commits to a 30-episode series, films 5
episodes, and the premise dies after episode 2.

**Why it fails.** A series only works if the premise is durable. A
premise that sounds great in a brainstorm often doesn't sustain across
multiple episodes when the audience has seen the pattern.

**Fix.** Test the premise as 1 to 2 standalone shorts before
committing to 30 episodes. If both perform, the premise is durable.
If they flop or the audience seems indifferent, the series wouldn't
have landed either.
