# Mining

Mining is where ideas come from when the user has exhausted what's in
their head. Every prolific creator runs at least one mining loop on a
schedule. The point is to stop relying on recall and start drawing from
queryable sources that the audience has already pre-validated.

Treat mining as a research session, not a creative one. Set a timer.
Pull raw material. Process into ideas after, never in the same sitting.

## Comment and DM mining (start here, this is the highest-signal source)

Comments on your own top posts are pre-validated questions from the exact
audience that engaged with the topic. Re-answering a top comment as its
own short tends to land in-niche because the demand was already
demonstrated.

The loop:

1. Open the last 3 posts that performed above your account average.
2. Sort comments by top.
3. Pull every comment that is a question, an objection, or a "wait but
   what about…" follow-up. These are video ideas verbatim.
4. Pull every comment that uses unexpected language for your topic. The
   audience's words tend to be better hooks than your own.

DMs are even higher signal and most creators ignore them entirely. A DM
that says "wait, can you explain X?" is one viewer telling you the gap
in the video they just watched. Re-shoot the answer as its own short.
Keep a running file of DM questions and process it weekly.

## Reddit mining (where the audience says what they actually think)

Reddit's value is pseudonymity: users post their most unfiltered and
honest thoughts. Pain points and word-for-word language in the right
subreddit cannot be fabricated and almost never read as marketing.

The audience-first search pattern, not the topic-first one:

1. Pick the audience subreddit, not the topic subreddit. ("r/espresso"
   is topic; "r/coffee" with "first machine" filter is audience.)
2. Sort by "top, all time." Read the first 20 to 30 post titles.
3. Open the 5 that overlap your pillars. Read the top comments, not
   the post body. The comments are where the recurring complaints and
   "how do I…" questions surface.
4. Cross-validate. When the same pain point shows up in both a relevant
   subreddit and a relevant comment thread on YouTube or LinkedIn, that's
   the signal it's a real demand surface, not a single weirdo.

A note on tools: third-party Reddit search tools come and go as the
Reddit API terms shift. Reddit's native search ("top, all time" inside
a sub) works for the audience-first loop above without any tool. Tools
help with scale, not with the underlying signal.

## Search and autocomplete mining (where the long tail lives)

Autocomplete is real search data ranked by popularity. The top
suggestion is the most-searched completion of the seed phrase. Every
completion is a candidate video title.

The alphabet trick on YouTube autocomplete:

1. Pick a seed phrase tied to one pillar. ("Home espresso", "first car",
   "Notion for writers".)
2. Type the seed + space + "a." Note the suggestions.
3. Repeat through the alphabet. 26 different completion sets, often
   surfacing questions you'd never have thought to ask.
4. Cross-check the strongest ones against Google "People Also Ask" boxes
   to confirm they're real demand, not autocomplete noise.

Google's "People Also Ask" panels are pulled from Google's question
index and ordered by priority. Each PAA tree maps the question hierarchy
around a seed. Answer The Public does the same in a visual wheel.

Filter: ignore any autocomplete that doesn't pass a "would a real viewer
search this on purpose?" gut check. Some completions are search artifacts,
not demand.

## Competitor outlier study (where the format opportunities are)

An outlier is a video on a channel that beats the channel's own baseline
by roughly 3 to 4x or more. Outliers point to a repeatable format the
channel has discovered. They are the cleanest signal in any niche.

The study process:

1. Pick 5 competitors at roughly your size or one tier above.
2. Sort each channel's videos by views, scan for outliers. (The 3 to 4x
   rule: if the channel averages 10k views and a video has 40k+, it's
   an outlier.)
3. For each outlier, ask Galloway's two questions: what made this
   unique? What about the packaging made it special?
4. Extract the mechanic, not the surface. "Comparison" is a mechanic.
   "$10 espresso vs $1,000 espresso" is a surface. Mechanics transplant
   across niches; surfaces don't.
5. Account for survivorship bias before generalizing. One outlier is a
   data point. The same mechanic working across 3 to 5 competitors is
   a format you can adapt.

Use outliers to study format and packaging, never to copy specific
videos. See `anti-patterns.md` for why copying frame-for-frame tends to
underperform.

## Podcast, interview, and review mining (where the language is unfiltered)

Run podcast Q&A segments through transcript tools (Descript, Otter,
Tactiq). Every question a guest gets asked is a question your audience
also has. Each answer segment is a candidate short.

App store reviews, Amazon reviews, and Trustpilot for products in your
niche are unfiltered audience language about what works and what
doesn't. Pure swipe-file fuel for hooks, especially the negative
reviews, which tend to surface the unmet needs your content can fill.

The shape of a useful review mine: 30 minutes, one product, sort by
"most recent" and by "most critical." Pull the lines that name a
specific frustration. Each one is the seed of a video.

## Swipe files (the long-term version of all of the above)

A swipe file is a curated collection of high-performing content,
hooks, formats, and references you can pull from when you sit down to
ideate. The term traces to Gary Halbert's physical folders of sales
letters that worked. The modern version is a Notion database or a
shared folder with tags.

The practical habit: 10 minutes a day actively looking for great
examples. Save anything that makes you stop and think "that's clever."
Tag by theme, platform, and content type so you can recombine.

Process rule: a swipe file is worthless without periodic review. Once a
week, open it, re-read what you saved, and surface the 3 to 5 items that
suggest video ideas you'd actually film. The review step is what turns
saved material into ideas. See Tiago Forte's Distill point in
`anti-patterns.md` for why the saving alone doesn't do it.

## The capture stack (so the inputs don't get lost)

Two-mode rule: fast capture and deeper processing are different jobs.
Mixing them kills both.

- **Fast capture.** Apple Notes, voice memo, Telegram-to-self, an iOS
  shortcut into a Notion inbox. Sub-5-second from idea to saved, or
  it doesn't happen in real life.
- **Deeper processing.** A Notion database (or equivalent) with fields
  for topic, angle, format, status. Triaged in batches, not in real time.

The handoff between the two is the part most creators get wrong. Build
the pipeline so capture goes straight into the processing inbox without
manual copying. See `assets/idea-tracker.md` for the minimum-viable
pipeline.

## A weekly mining loop (do this on a schedule)

`assets/mining-checklist.md` is the printable version. The core loop:

- Top comments on your last 3 posts.
- 2 audience subreddits, sorted top of all time, scan top 30 titles.
- A 26-letter autocomplete pass on one seed phrase.
- 3 competitor outliers studied for format, not topic.
- Add the strongest 5 to 10 captures to the idea inbox.

30 to 45 minutes a week, every week. The compounding is the point.
