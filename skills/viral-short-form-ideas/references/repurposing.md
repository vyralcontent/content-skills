# Repurposing

Repurposing is how prolific creators post 30+ pieces a week without
generating 30+ ideas a week. One strong source asset, many derived
surfaces. The skill is structural, not creative: figure out the trunk,
identify the branches, then run the decomposition on a schedule.

This is the engine behind every "I post 250 pieces a week" claim you've
seen. It also fixes the most common idea-shortage problem, which is
usually a repurposing failure, not an ideation failure.

## The content tree (the mental model)

A tree with one high-value asset at the trunk and many derived pieces
as branches. The trunk is expensive to make once. Each branch is cheap.

- **Trunk candidates.** Long-form video, deep written guide, podcast
  episode, webinar, live workshop. One asset deep enough to carry a
  topic completely.
- **Branches.** Short-form clips, infographics, visual quotes, threads,
  carousels, screenshots, single-frame stills, audiograms.
- **Destination.** Every branch links back to the trunk. The dispersed
  pieces compound into one converting funnel instead of evaporating
  into the feed.

One trunk asset typically spawns 20+ derived pieces over a few weeks.
A serious repurposing creator runs maybe 1 to 4 trunk assets a month
and lives off the branches.

## The Hormozi engine (test cheap, then scale the winners)

The principle: ideas earn their way up the production cost ladder. A
tweet is the cheapest possible test. A polished 30-second short is
roughly 10x more expensive in time. A long-form video is 100x.

The loop:

1. **Daily idea dump on the cheapest format.** A tweet, a single-line
   post, a thread. No production, just the idea in writing.
2. **Weekly review.** Roughly an hour. Which posts earned engagement?
   The winners are the only candidates for the next tier.
3. **Promote winners.** A winning tweet becomes a short-form script. A
   winning short becomes a long-form video. Each tier costs more, so
   only proven ideas earn the spend.
4. **Decompose long-form.** A successful long-form piece becomes 30+
   platform-specific shorts, carousels, and posts over the following
   weeks.

The discipline is in the test step, not the production step. Most
creators invert the ratio and lose weeks producing polished shorts of
ideas that wouldn't have survived a one-line test.

## The one-topic-many-angles pass (Ship 30's angle set, applied)

When the user has one strong topic and wants 5 non-repeating angles,
run it through the angle set from `frameworks.md`:

- **Consumption-based.** What you just watched, read, or used about
  this topic.
- **Response-based.** A reaction to or extension of someone else's
  take.
- **Curation.** A roundup of the best resources, examples, or tactics.
- **Instructional.** A how-to on a sub-skill inside the topic.
- **Narrative.** A personal lesson built around the topic.

Take a topic like "first home espresso machine." Five outputs:

- Consumption: "I just rewatched James Hoffmann's beginner machine
  video. Here's the one thing I think he's wrong about."
- Response: "Everyone says start with a Gaggia. They're wrong, and
  here's why."
- Curation: "The 3 espresso channels that taught me everything I know."
- Instructional: "How to dial in your first shot in under 10 minutes."
- Narrative: "I spent $2,400 on espresso before realizing I only
  needed $180 of it."

Same topic, five completely different pieces. None of them are
variations; each is a distinct angle.

## The podcast-to-shorts pattern (the highest-yield trunk)

A 30-minute podcast tends to produce 10 to 20 viable shorts. A 2-hour
episode tends to produce 30 to 50+. The bottleneck is finding the
"wow moments" in the transcript, not generating ideas from scratch.

The process:

1. **Full transcript.** Descript, Otter, Tactiq, or any of the
   auto-clipper transcription tools.
2. **Mark wow moments.** Every bold claim, surprising stat, funny
   line, contrarian take, or "I've never told this story before" beat.
3. **Each marked moment becomes a 30 to 60 second short.** A new hook
   recorded over the top, or surfaced as on-screen text, then the
   relevant clip below.
4. **Spread the publish schedule.** One episode can carry a creator's
   short-form output for 3 to 6 weeks.

Auto-clipper tools (OpusClip, Flowjin, Choppity) automate the
transcription and rough-cut steps. They still require human selection
for the actual viral ones, so don't trust the tool's auto-suggestions
without reviewing them.

## Series vs one-off (the structure that's harder to get bored of)

Series-first creators generate fewer "ideas" per week but produce more
videos. One premise carries 5 to 30 episodes.

Why series tend to win on short-form:

- **Completion compounds.** A series episode that ends mid-tension
  (cliffhanger, "part 2 tomorrow") tends to improve the current
  episode's completion rate, which is the primary distribution signal
  on every major short-form platform.
- **Idea efficiency.** A premise like "I'm going to taste every coffee
  drink at every chain in my city" yields 30+ episodes from one
  ideation decision.
- **Audience habit.** Series formats build the return-visit muscle that
  algorithmic feeds otherwise erode.

Meta has a native Series feature on Reels for grouping shorts into
seasons and episodes. Use it if the platform you're posting on supports
it, since native grouping tends to surface follow-on episodes to
viewers who finished the first.

Caveat: a series only works if the premise is durable. Test the premise
as one or two standalone shorts before committing to 30 episodes.

## Re-uploading and re-angling winners (the lowest-effort move available)

The cheapest source of new ideas is your own top-performing video.

The "same insight, new hook" pattern:

1. Pick your top-performing short from the last 6 months.
2. Rewrite only the first 2 to 3 seconds. Three new hook angles, drawn
   from `frameworks.md` and the hooks skill.
3. Post each as a new video over the following weeks.

The original hook is often not the best possible hook; it was just the
first one you tried. The new hook often outperforms the original.

Justin Welsh's republish rule: high-performing posts get republished at
roughly 6, 12, 18, and 24 months, with light tweaks. The audience has
turned over and the algorithm doesn't penalize. On TikTok specifically,
re-uploads from your own catalog with a different hook and on-screen
text tend to surface as fresh distribution candidates, and creators
often see "delayed explosion" months later on previously flopped videos.

## A practical repurposing plan

`assets/repurposing-plan.md` is the template. The shape:

- One trunk asset per week or per month.
- 4 to 8 derived shorts from the trunk.
- 2 to 3 carousels or visual-quote pieces.
- 1 round of re-uploads on past winners.
- Every branch links back to the trunk somewhere in the funnel.

Most creators underestimate how many derived pieces one trunk asset
can carry. Push the user past their comfort number. The first 5
derived pieces are easy; the next 15 are where the real leverage is.
