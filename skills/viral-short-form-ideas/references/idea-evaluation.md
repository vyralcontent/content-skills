# Idea evaluation

Generating ideas is the easy part. Most creators have more ideas than
they can produce. The leverage is in filtering, so the ones that get
made are the ones most likely to land. Filter before you produce, not
after.

This reference is the filter pass. Run it on a list of candidate ideas
before any of them earn a script.

## The hook-conception test (do this first, cut fast)

If you can't conceive a strong hook for an idea in 60 seconds, the idea
is probably weak. Hooks aren't ornaments on a finished idea; they are a
screening filter for whether the idea was actually viable.

The test:

1. State the idea in one sentence.
2. Try to write 3 hook options for it in 60 seconds.
3. If all 3 feel flat, the idea is the problem, not the hooks. Cut it.
4. If even one feels sharp, the idea passes this stage. Move on.

Both Paddy Galloway's funnel and the MrBeast memo put title and
thumbnail (the hook equivalent) validity inside the idea-evaluation
phase, not the production phase. The order matters: evaluate the
package first, build the video to deliver it second.

For hook writing itself, see the `viral-hooks` skill or
`viral-short-form/references/hooks.md`.

## CCN fit (does the idea serve all three audiences)

Galloway's filter. Every video on a channel is shown to three groups:

- **Core.** Subscribers who watch most of what you post. They want
  more of the thing they signed up for.
- **Casual.** People who've watched you before but don't subscribe.
  They need a reason to click again.
- **New.** People who've never seen you. They need the hook to do all
  the work; they don't know your context.

A strong idea serves at least two of the three. A great idea serves
all three. An idea that serves only Core (an inside-baseball reference,
a callback to a previous video) is a fan-service piece. Fine
occasionally, dangerous as a default.

The check: for each candidate, name which of C/C/N it serves. If it
only serves Core, demote it. If it serves only New, ask whether the
existing audience will feel left behind.

## The quantitative filter (MrBeast's four scores)

From the leaked production memo. Score each candidate idea against:

- **CTR.** How likely is a viewer to click the thumbnail and title?
  On short-form: how likely is a viewer to stop scrolling on the first
  frame and on-screen hook?
- **AVD.** Predicted average view duration. How long will the average
  viewer stay?
- **AVP.** Predicted average view percentage. What fraction of the
  video will the average viewer watch?
- **Wow factor.** Gut excitement. The only qualitative metric.

Score each on a 1 to 10 scale. Be honest. An idea scoring 6 6 6 9 is a
different bet than one scoring 9 9 9 4. The first might surprise. The
second is a polished idea no one cares about.

Two rules that come with this filter:

- **Redundancy.** If an idea is 50/50 to land, write a backup. Every
  load-bearing component of an idea gets over-engineered.
- **Never coin-flip a critical component.** The hook, the payoff, and
  the format are the load-bearing parts. They each need to clear the
  bar independently.

## The gut-excitement gate (the final filter)

The MrBeast memo's most-quoted line, paraphrased: if you're not
excited to film it, the video is dead before it starts. The personal
excitement gate is the last filter, and it overrides the others.

The check:

1. Imagine pressing record on this video tomorrow morning.
2. If the feeling is "good, let's go," it passes.
3. If the feeling is "I should," it fails, regardless of score.

This isn't fluffy. A flat creator energy reads on camera and tanks the
hook delivery, which kills the retention, which kills distribution. A
6/10 idea you're excited about beats an 8/10 idea you're not.

## The backup-idea rule

Adapted from the redundancy principle. Every production slot in your
calendar holds two candidates, not one. The first is the lead. The
second is the backup.

When the lead idea hits a wall (the hook doesn't land in rehearsal, the
shoot environment doesn't work, the energy is off), the backup is
ready. Most creators don't carry a backup, so when the lead falls
apart they ship something half-baked anyway. The backup is the fix.

This also forces evaluation discipline: if you can't generate a
backup as strong as the lead, the lead probably isn't as strong as
you think.

## Tiago Forte's Distill step (where most ideas die anyway)

The capture system fails when ideas stay raw. A captured note like
"video about morning routines" is inert. It survives in the inbox
forever but never becomes a video.

The Distill step: rewrite each captured idea into the format a
producer would need. At minimum:

- **Hook line.** The actual first sentence of the video.
- **Payoff.** The one thing the viewer walks away with.
- **Format.** Talking head, demo, before/after, listicle, narrative.
- **Length target.** 15 / 30 / 60 / 90 seconds.

A captured idea that survives a Distill pass is a real candidate. One
that doesn't was never an idea, it was a topic.

This is the step most creators skip and it's why most creators have a
thousand ideas saved and nothing to post. See `anti-patterns.md` for
the longer treatment of the idea-wall problem.

## A practical filter pass on a list

Run this on any list of 20+ candidate ideas before any of them earn a
script:

1. **Hook test on each.** Cut anything where you can't write a sharp
   hook in 60 seconds. Often kills 30 to 50% of the list immediately.
2. **CCN tag.** Mark each surviving candidate C, CC, or CCN based on
   who it serves. Demote Core-only.
3. **Quantitative score.** CTR / AVD / AVP / Wow on a 1 to 10 scale.
   Skip this for ideas you're producing for fun; do it for ideas
   you're banking on.
4. **Excitement gate.** For the top 5 by score, run the "press record
   tomorrow" check. Cut anything that flunks.
5. **Distill the survivors.** Hook line, payoff, format, length for
   each. The list that comes out the other side is what you actually
   produce.

A typical list of 30 raw ideas survives this pass as 5 to 8 production
candidates. That's the right ratio. If 25 of 30 survive, your filter
isn't strict enough.

## When evaluation gets in the way

Don't run this filter on a brainstorm in progress. Generate first,
filter second. Mixing modes kills both. The filter pass is a separate
session, ideally a day or two after the generation pass, so you can
read the candidates with fresh eyes.
